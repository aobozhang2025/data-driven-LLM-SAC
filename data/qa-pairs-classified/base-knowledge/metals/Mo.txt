Chemical Properties of Molybdenum (Mo)

answer
Molybdenum, with an atomic number of 42 and an electron configuration of [Kr] 4d⁵ 5s¹, possesses several key chemical characteristics relevant to its catalytic activity:

Variable Oxidation States: Molybdenum can exist in a wide range of oxidation states, from -2 to +6. The most common and stable states are +4 and +6. This flexibility allows it to participate in a variety of redox reactions, a crucial aspect of catalysis, by facilitating electron transfer.
Electronegativity: On the Pauling scale, molybdenum has an electronegativity of 2.16. This moderate electronegativity allows it to form stable bonds with a variety of elements, including carbon, nitrogen, oxygen, and sulfur, which are common components of catalyst supports and reactants.
Coordination Chemistry: Molybdenum atoms can coordinate with various ligands, forming diverse and stable complexes. In single-atom catalysis, this ability is critical for anchoring the individual Mo atoms to the support material and for interacting with reactant molecules. The geometry and nature of these coordination environments can be tailored to fine-tune the catalyst's selectivity and activity.
Reactivity: While relatively unreactive with water and oxygen at room temperature, molybdenum's reactivity increases at higher temperatures. It readily forms molybdenum disulfide (MoS₂) and molybdenum trioxide (MoO₃), which are themselves important catalytic materials and precursors for single-atom catalysts.

This is a widely used, water-soluble, and relatively inexpensive Mo precursor. It can be easily impregnated onto various support materials and then thermally decomposed to form molybdenum oxide species, which can subsequently be reduced to single Mo atoms.
