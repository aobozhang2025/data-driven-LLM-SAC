question
best practices in Pt SAC synthesis

answer
pH control is critical: acidic media favors stable Pt-chloride species; basic media may cause hydrolysis.
Slow reduction helps isolate single atoms rather than clusters.
Ligand assistance (e.g., using amine or phosphine ligands) can stabilize single Pt atoms on supports.
Support pre-treatment enhances anchoring and dispersion (e.g., introducing oxygen vacancies or heteroatoms).
Avoiding agglomeration is essential: control calcination and reduction conditions carefully.

question
precautions when using Cu precursors in SAC synthesis

Selection criteria:

Avoid precursors with aggressive anions (e.g., Cl⁻) if they interfere with support or poison active sites.

Precursor handling:
Store in airtight, moisture-free conditions.

Apply slow drying and careful calcination (e.g., 150–300 °C) to maintain atom dispersion.

Safety precautions:
Always work in a fume hood.
Wear PPE: gloves, lab coat, eye protection.
Avoid mixing with reducing agents unless under controlled atmosphere.

question
selection considerations of Ru precursor in SAC synthesis

answer
Solubility: For solution-based methods, water-soluble precursors (e.g., RuCl₃, Ru(NO)(NO₃)₃) are preferred
Thermal behavior: Must decompose cleanly to Ru(0) or RuOx without forming aggregates
Ligand volatility: Ligands (e.g., CO, acac) must be easily removed without residue
Metal dispersion: Chelated or organometallic precursors may improve dispersion as single atoms
Safety: Some Ru compounds (e.g., RuO₄, NO-containing) are highly toxic and volatile

question
General Safety Precautions for Ru Precursors in SAC synthesis

answer
Always use glove box or Schlenk line for air-sensitive organometallics.
Protect from light and heat (especially NO, CO containing species).
Avoid exposure to moisture for hygroscopic chlorides.
Ensure fume hood use to avoid inhalation of NO, CO, or RuO₄ vapors.
Decompose Ru precursors in a controlled reducing/oxidizing atmosphere (H₂/Ar, O₂/air) to prevent sintering or unwanted oxide formation.

question
Key Considerations for Using Ir Precursors in SAC synthesis

answer
Stability: Organometallic precursors like Ir(acac)₃ are less stable than chlorides, but often give better dispersion.
Volatility: Crucial for ALD/CVD methods (Ir(acac)₃, Ir(COD)(acac)).
Solubility: Important for wet methods—chlorides and acids are water-soluble; organometallics dissolve in ethanol, acetone, etc.
Decomposition: Need to decompose cleanly to Ir(0) or Irδ⁺; residual ligands can poison active sites.
Anchorability: Ligand type affects metal-support interaction; Cl⁻ can compete with surface anchoring.
Safety: Many Ir compounds are toxic, corrosive, and oxidizing; handle with gloves and in fume hoods.

question
General Notes on Dy Precursors in SAC

answer
Solubility and Stability: Dy salts like nitrates and chlorides are generally water-soluble, making them suitable for wet impregnation and co-precipitation. Organometallic precursors like Dy(acac)₃ are used when organic solvents or vapor-phase deposition is preferred.

Handling: Many Dy salts are hygroscopic and/or sensitive to moisture, so proper storage (airtight containers, desiccators) is important. Use personal protective equipment (PPE) to avoid exposure.

Toxicity: While Dy compounds are not extremely toxic, they can cause irritation or sensitization. Avoid inhalation, ingestion, and prolonged skin contact.

Thermal Treatment: Many SAC syntheses require calcination or reduction steps; Dy precursors should decompose cleanly without leaving undesirable residues.
